## Raw Extraction Snapshot

Global Economics Update | 2nd April 2026 The indirect inflation effects of higher energy prices • Beyond their direct effects on consumer prices of fuels and piped gas , higher crude oil and natural gas prices will prompt firms to pass on higher fuel costs to consumers, boosting the prices of non -fuel items of the CPI such as electricity and food . In our baseline scenario, we estimate that these indirect effects on inflation – which are separate from “second -round” effects via broader wage - and price -setting – could range from 0.7% -pt in the US to nearly 1.5% -pt in the euro -zone, with the UK and Japan in between. • Chart 1 provides a framework for thinking about how a surge in oil and (natural) gas prices feed through to consumer prices . “First-round” effects are one-off price adjustments to higher fuel costs . “Second-round” effects capture workers and firms respond ing to first-round price increases by bidding up wages and prices to defend their real incomes. On their own, the first -round effects are (for want of a better word ) transitory, even if they can take time to fully feed through . But when inflation expectations rise amid conditions of excess demand , would -be transitory price effects can become self -sustaining via second -round effects.
