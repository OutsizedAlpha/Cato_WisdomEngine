Fundamental Context
Health care is a classically defensive sector in times of economic disruption and slowdown. The sector
has several fundamental characteristics that make demand for its products and services relatively stable
regardless of economic conditions and geopolitical uncertainties. Medical care, prescription drugs, and
treatments are necessities rather than discretionary purchases—people continue to need health care when
sick or managing chronic conditions, whether the economy is strong or weak. This inelastic demand is
further reinforced by insurance coverage and government health care programs (Medicare, Medicaid) that
buffer consumer affordability concerns, in our view. As a result, health care companies typically generate
predictable, recurring revenues with lower correlation to GDP growth, making their stocks less volatile during
economic downturns and attractive safe havens for investors seeking portfolio stability during periods of
market uncertainty. Therefore, we see select companies in the sector with healthy revenue streams and
solid balance sheets as good investment alternatives to navigate the current market volatility, but also as
compelling long-term investment opportunities.

We view a prolonged oil shock as a major risk to the economy, while the duration of the crisis is highly
uncertain. Historically, we have seen examples of oil price shocks causing economic recessions and bear
markets for U.S. equity indices. However, it remains unclear whether the current environment of higher
energy prices will be short-lived or persist for several months. Though we do not see an elevated oil price
environment benefiting the sector through an acceleration of sales or profitability, we note a few areas that
could remain well-positioned to outperform peers via competitive advantages and more defensive business
models.

Sub-Industry Views: Biopharma
By nature, pharmaceuticals are a defensive sub-industry, showing more resilience during economic
downturns, as the need to treat illnesses does not stop. The demand for essential medicines, particularly in
the fields of oncology, immunology, and mental health, will remain strong, in our view, as they are seen as
necessary treatments. During recessions, the demand for essential drugs remains stable and the price
elasticity of specialty drugs tends to be low. These medicines are viewed as critical for patients who depend
on them to live their daily lives, a trend we expect to remain unchanged in case of a recession or market
turbulence. In that sense, pharmaceutical companies that manufacture essential drugs are well-positioned
during economic downturns as they have less exposure to wider economic uncertainty compared to
companies in cyclical industries.
We expect strong growth in worldwide prescription drugs sales for the next five years. According to
health care data provider Evaluate Pharma, worldwide prescription drug sales are expected to post a
compound annual growth rate of 7.0% between 2025 and 2032 and reach nearly $2.0 trillion by 2032.
Evaluate expects Eli Lilly (LLY 1,008 *****) and AbbVie (ABBV 228 ****) to be the top-two prescription
drug companies by 2032.

Oncology will continue to experience significant growth in the next few years, in our view. This is driven,
in our view, by the increasing number of cancer patients worldwide as a result of changing lifestyles,
urbanization, and increasing life expectancies in recent decades. According to estimates from the American
Cancer Society, there were 2 million new cancer cases and almost 609,820 deaths from cancer in the U.S. in
2023. By 2050, the global burden is expected to grow to over 35 million new cancer cases. Between 2024
and 2030, oncology drug sales are expected to post a CAGR of 10% and make up 21% of the total
prescription and OTC drugs worldwide in 2030, according to Evaluate Pharma. Oncology therapies have
generated high revenues and strong margins, a trend we expect to continue.
Moreover, we expect the trend of aging populations to continue to positively impact the demand for
prescription drugs in 2026 and beyond, especially in key therapeutic areas such as immune-oncology,
endocrinology, cardiology, and neurology. According to the latest U.S. census data, the 65+ age cohort is
growing five times as fast as the total U.S. population. Within our U.S. Pharmaceuticals coverage, AbbVie,
Amgen (AMGN 379 ****), Bristol-Myers Squibb (BMY 61 ****), Eli Lilly, and Merck & Co. (MRK 119 ****) have
large exposures to these therapeutic areas in their portfolios and are expected to continue to benefit from
the growing demand for key drugs in these fields.
Drug Distributors, Life Science Tools and Services, and Health Care Services
We view pharmaceutical distribution as one of the most defensive areas within the health care sector.
Pharmaceuticals are an essential product with reliable demand across strong and weak economic periods.
We also think aging populations in developed markets and economic growth in emerging markets will drive
long-term demand for drug therapies. Barriers to entry are significant, with the “Big Three” of McKesson
(MCK 933 ****), Cencora (COR 354 ****), and Cardinal Health (CAH 219 ****) estimated to control over
90% of the U.S. drug distribution market. Historically, we have seen pharmaceutical drug distributors
outperform with their fee-for-service model, which supports the ability to pass through price increases and
maintain margins despite inflationary environments and other economic disruption, most recently seen in the
record U.S. inflationary period of 2021-2022. Relative to peers, we note that CAH maintains relatively more
exposure to inflationary energy/transportation costs through its Global Medical Products and Distribution

We see opportunities in service-based companies with less energy-intensive operations. Within our Life
Science Tools and Services coverage, we feel the service-based business IQVIA (IQV 174 ****) may be well
positioned to navigate a higher energy cost environment. The company is a contract research organization
(CRO) and health care information/tech services provider that is not exposed to manufacturing or product
distribution. We see near-term sales visibility from the Research & Development Solutions segment's $32.7
billion contracted backlog (on a trailing 12-month basis as of Q4 2025 earnings). We also see growing
decentralized clinical trial (DCT) capabilities, which utilize virtual and telemedicine technologies, as a plus in
an environment of higher transportation costs.
Essential disease treatments are likely to hold up better within a weaker economy, in our view. We
consider DaVita (DVA 153 ***), within our Health Care Services coverage, to be a highly defensive name
providing an essential service (dialysis treatment for kidney failure) that should see healthy demand despite
a higher energy cost environment or even an economic slowdown. While higher oil prices could hamper
demand by increasing the cost of patient transportation to dialysis centers, we think the impact may be
limited by Medicaid coverage for non-emergency medical transportation (NEMT) as well as DVA's growing
home dialysis offerings.
