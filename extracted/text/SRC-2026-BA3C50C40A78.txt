Global Markets Chart Pack - Overview
The spike in energy prices stemming from the conflict in the Middle East has ignited concerns of a stagflationary shock and put global equities and government bonds under
severe pressure over the past month. However, hopes of a swift end to the conflict has spurred a recovery in recent days. In our baseline scenario, in which traffic through the
Strait of Hormuz resumes by the end of April and oil prices fall back, we expect the AI-driven surge in equity markets to restart, with tech-heavy stock indices in the US and
Asia outperforming. Meanwhile, government bonds such as Gilts that have suffered severely over the past month will likely rally by the most. If the conflict reignites,
however, bonds and equities would likely come under renewed pressure, as tighter monetary policy combines with lower growth, but the dollar would likely rally further.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Equities - Overview
Equity markets around the world have sold off over the past month as the conflict in the Middle East has pushed up energy prices and raised the prospect of a return of
stagflation. Stock markets of net energy exporting countries in Latin America and North America have generally fared less poorly than those of net importers. However,
equities have rebounded in recent days and if the conflict continues to de-escalate, we anticipate energy-heavy equity indices will lag in the recovery as tech-heavy equity
indices fare better. If the war drags on, however, equity markets globally would likely come under more pressure and we imagine that, for a while at least, the geographic
patterns of relative performance seen recently would endure.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Equities - Developed Markets
European and Japanese stock markets had borne the brunt of the deterioration in global risk sentiment since the war began, whereas equities in countries such as the US
whose economies are less exposed to the conflict had been insulated somewhat. But if the conflict does not last for too much longer, as is our base case, we suspect
European and Japanese stock markets would continue to recover over the rest of the year. Even so, we expect US equity outperformance to persist amid renewed investor
enthusiasm for AI-related stocks.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Equities - Emerging Markets
EM equities sold off more sharply than those in DMs during the recent market turmoil. The markets that had rallied the most over the past year or so have slumped the most
over the past month, a move that is hard to justify solely by rising energy prices and may also reflect some speculative froth coming out of markets such as Korea and South
Africa. However, hopes of an end to the conflict have supported a recovery in EM equity markets, particularly tech-heavy markets in Asia, such as Korea and Taiwan. We think
these stock markets have further to benefit from AI enthusiasm and will outperform other EM indices over the rest of the year, while a drop back in commodity prices may
prove a headwind for those in Latin America.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Bonds - Overview
Government bonds in the UK and euro-zone have come under the most pressure since the war in the Middle East began, whereas Treasuries haven’t suffered nearly as much.
If the conflict continues to de-escalate then the rally in DM government bonds over recent days is likely to continue in most cases as investors row back on expectations of
interest rate hikes. That said, we don’t think Treasuries have much further to rally given money markets are no longer pricing in interest rate hikes. And in a world in which the
war re-escalates, bonds would likely come under renewed pressure, as central banks hike interest rates and the need for fiscal support drives up term premia.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Bonds - DM Government
DM government bond yields have risen since the war broke out as investors have shifted from anticipating interest rate cuts this year to discounting interest rate hikes in
most places. Reflecting this, shorter-dated government bond yields have risen by more than long-dated government bond yields, culminated in a bear flattening of DM yield
curves. As a result, if the conflict continues to de-escalate, we expect short-dated government bond yields would drop back more sharply than long-dated ones as investors
unwind their bets on interest rate hikes.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Bonds - DM Term & Risk Premia
The fact that yield curves have mostly flattened – even in Europe – is perhaps evidence that the rise in yields is primarily reflective of an upward shift in rate expectations
rather than rising term premia. Admittedly, the war does have the potential to hit governments’ fiscal positions: the rise in euro-zone bond spreads over the war is one sign
investors are starting to get concerned. But we think governments will generally take a fairly cautious approach to fiscal policy in response to the war, in part because they
have limited fiscal space in most cases. It could, nonetheless, expose some of those that came into it in more precarious positions, and it wouldn’t be surprising to see euro-
zone spreads rise a bit further.

While option‑adjusted spreads on the FTSE Corporate Bond Indices remain tight by historical standards, they have widened over the past month, which perhaps reflect
concerns about the impact on economic activity from the war in the Middle East. Spreads may rise further if the conflict rumbles on and investors’ concerns about how it may
dampen growth grow but if something akin to our baseline scenario materialises then we expect spreads in Europe and the US will drop back as risk sentiment recovers and
the economic impact from the conflict is contained.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Bonds - DM Policy Rates
The jump in energy prices since the war broke out has resulted in a large upward swing in investors’ near-term interest rate expectations in most DMs. The biggest shift has
come in the UK, where investors had been expecting quite a bit more monetary easing before war broke out, but rising energy prices and a hawkish tone struck by the MPC
has put an end to those chances and investors now anticipate rate hikes. But we think the chances of policy tightening in the UK are overdone, and in our baseline scenario,
in which inflation concerns ease as energy prices fall, we expect interest rate expectations to decline further.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Bonds - EM Local-Currency Government
EM local-currency government bond yields have risen in most places as investors’ expectations for monetary easing have been scaled back. The biggest moves have come in
parts of Latin America and Emerging Europe rather than in EM Asia, where easing cycles had already largely ended or were much more advanced than in the other regions.
China is an outlier, with yields on its government bonds falling over the past month, likely reflecting the relative insulation from the conflict. We expect EM local-currency
government bond yields to decline over the rest of the year as inflation concerns ease and the prospect of interest rate hikes dissipates in most places.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Bonds - EM Dollar
EM dollar bond spreads have risen over the past month. Unsurprisingly, spreads have increased the most in Africa and Europe, given their dependence on energy imports,
followed by the Middle East which has been directly involved in the war. We suspect EM dollar bond spreads will fall back as the conflict de-escalates, but given they remain
close to multi-year low we don’t think they have far to fall. However, a prolonged conflict would likely see spreads continue to widen. Meanwhile, upcoming elections in
Brazil, Colombia and Hungary are likely to be closely contested and potential flashpoints for dollar bond spreads.

Global Markets Chart Pack - FX - Overview
Safe‑haven demand and a US-positive terms of trade shock has boosted the dollar against all major currencies since the outbreak of war. We think that the upside for the US
dollar is limited if the conflict continues to de-escalate as risk appetite improves and the favourable shift in the US terms of trade unwinds. But tailwinds from a shift in yield
gaps in the dollar's favour and further US equity outperformance should still see it rally over the course of the year. But if the war were to rumble on, we think there is more
scope for the dollar to rally as it benefits from a further US-positive shift in the terms of trade and increased demand for safe-havens.

Global Markets Chart Pack - FX - Relative Yields
The dollar has diverged somewhat from interest rate differentials thanks to increased demand for safe-havens and a favorable terms of trade shocks. Nominal rate
differentials may have moved in favour of the euro and the pound, but in real terms, which factors in the larger jump in inflation expectations in the eurozone/UK vis-a-vis
the US, they have shifted in the dollar’s favour. Yield differentials are likely to support the dollar this year, as we think the Fed will keep its policy rate on hold, whereas
markets are pricing in some easing. At the same time, we doubt central banks in other DMs will deliver the amount of policy tightening which is priced into money markets.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Commodities - Overview
Oil prices have surged over the past month thanks to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and damage to energy infrastructure. Under our baseline scenario, in which
shipments through the Strait resume by the end of the month, and any damage to oil-related infrastructure is limited and quickly repairable, oil exports recover fully in 2027.
As a result, we expect the price of Brent crude to ease back this year and fall to its pre-war level by end-2027. That said, longer-lasting disruptions and more extensive
damage to infrastructure would mean oil prices are in triple-digit territory both this year and next year.

Global Markets Chart Pack - Commodities - Copper & Gold
Gold has acted more like a risky asset than a safe-haven over the course of the conflict as its price has fallen even as geopolitical uncertainty has been ratcheted up. We
suspect weaker structural demand for gold has left its price vulnerable to a pick-up in real yields and reduced risk appetite. Although gold prices have staged a bit of a
recovery in recent days, perhaps on reports that the conflict may soon be over, we don’t think this is a sign of things to come. We think there is more exuberance to come
out of the market which could trigger further price falls this year.

The conflict in Iran has increased uncertainty and pushed up market interest rates raising concerns of a slowing in activity in the property market. This has weighed heavily
on REITs globally, which have underperformed broader equity indices. In the US, although we doubt the conflict in Iran will have a lasting impact on the property market in
our baseline scenario, we still expect REITs to underperform the broader equity market this year.
