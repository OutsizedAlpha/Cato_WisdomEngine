Mapping the next stage of the AI rally
• While developments in the Middle East are the primary concern for markets right now , sentiment around
AI remains a key factor too. We still think that renewed AI enthusiasm will boos t stock markets this year.
• The S&P 500 “ big-tech” sectors have fared better than most since the war began . But it has nonetheless
been a stiff headwind for them and, thanks in part to earlier turmoil in the software sector , they have now
fallen further form their recent peaks than other sectors. That said, we don’t think either of these headwinds
fundamentally undermine the broader optimism around AI that had boosted these sectors in recent years.
• Investors seem to agree that the conflict has not darkened the outlook for tech earnings (which had largely
driven the rally in recent years). Indeed, analyst earnings forecasts have continued to soar in recent weeks.
And they also seem to share our view that the turmoil in the software sector is not necessarily bad news for
other tech stocks, with AI -related stocks excluding software ones holding up well even as software sold off .
• We would go further and say that the disruption facing the software industry is a net positive for the stock
market. Of course, AI will create losses for some and may cause financial dislocation as those losses wash
through the system. But it will create gains for others too. This is the historical pattern with general -purpose
technologies , and we think the net benefits for the S&P 500 will outweigh the losses over the medium term .
• Stepping back, we have not seen anything to change our mind that AI -related stocks have further upside.
• We have also not seen anything to make us think that the main beneficiaries of renewed AI hype would
be different to those that have benefited so far . Both our macro and markets analysis suggest that diffusion
across economies is still at an early stage , with little sign of much broadening out over the past two years .
• Accordingly, we continue to think stocks in the US, China, Korea, Taiwan and Japan are best set to benefit
from AI enthusiasm. This is based on their exposures to “AI industries ” – which we identify by analysing
the composition of the largest AI ETFs by AUM as of start -2026 – and on our CE AI Economic Impact Index.
• We have recently revised down our end-2026 S&P 500 forecast given the assumed hit to global economic
activity from war in the Middle East . But our new forecast of 7,500 (down from 8,000) is still optimistic
and around the top of consensus forecasts. And we expect this rally to be led by stocks in AI industries .

Appendix
CE AI Equity Index Methodology
Identifying an AI stock…
To select our universe of AI stocks we defer to the judgement of major ETF providers. Specifically, our
indices use all stocks featured in the top 10 AI ETFs by AUM (as defined by ETF.com) at the start of 2026.
This approach does not mean every stock exposed to AI is considered. For example, Walmart is one stock
that has rallied strongly in part seemingly because of investor enthusiasm around its use of AI – but it does
not feature in any of these 10 ETFs. And there are plenty of stocks included that are increasingly seen as
losers from AI: a lot of software stocks, for example. The reality is the perceived winners from the A I
revolution are in constant flux, so no AI index will accurately gauge enthusiasm for the technology.
Still, we think this a better approach than simply using the combined weight of the IT, consumer
discretionary and communication services sectors. Granted, doing so makes sense for the US stock market
on a cap -weighted basis because these GICS sectors are home to the ‘Magnificent Seven’ and other major
US tech names. But it is not necessarily the case that the average US consumer discretionary firm is more
exposed to AI than the average US industrial firm, for example. At least, that is what the sector
compositions of these AI ETFs indicate. So, just using these three so -called “tech sectors” to quantify the
exposure of non -US markets to the AI theme leaves room for improvement , in our view. Likewise using
these sectors for US stocks doesn’t make much sense to us if you are looking on an equal -weighted basis.
Creating sector/industry sub -indices…
When it comes to creating AI sector/industry sub -indices, there are generally two approaches. The first
camp (e.g. many active ETFs/indices) takes a discretionary approach to identifying software firms, either
via proprietary screens or subjective decisions. The second camp relies on firm classifications made
according to the GICS (Global Industry Classification Standa rd) or similar. Under this approach, firms
involved in the software space may not feature in a software index if they are involved in other a ctivities.
This creates the confusing situation where most of the mega cap US tech firms – many of which are seen
to be involved in the software business in way or another – do not feature in software indices. Only
Microsoft is classed as a software stock. (Chart A 3, Appendix, shows which GICS industry these mega
caps sit in and their weight in each. It also shows the weight of each industry in the respective GICS
sector, and the weight of each sector in the MSCI USA Index).
For our CE AI equity Indices, we opt to use GICS classifications. Put simply, given the imperfections of
both approaches, we think that the objectivity of using accepted classifications makes the indices – and
the messages from them – easier to interpret.
We define “enablers” as firms in the S emiconductor industry group plus those in the Utilities and Real
Estate sectors. The remaining AI -related stocks are classed as “users” .
