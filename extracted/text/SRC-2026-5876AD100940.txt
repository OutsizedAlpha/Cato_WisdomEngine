Fundamental Context
The auto industry was already struggling with high inventory levels prior to the oil price spike. As of
January 30, U.S. new vehicle inventories stood at 98 days’ supply, according to Cox Automotive data, well
above the historical average of 60 days that is considered ideal by many industry observers. The February
data will be reported in the coming days, but we find the fact that inventory levels were so high prior to the
recent oil price spike very concerning. Higher oil prices are likely to raise recessionary concerns, hurt
consumer confidence, and negatively impact auto sales. A higher oil price environment could also re-stoke

inflation and negatively impact the outlook for interest rates, reversing a key tailwind that has helped boost
auto sales over the past several quarters.
We think F stands to lose the most from higher fuel prices. In 2025, trucks and SUVs accounted for 98.0%
of F’s total U.S. vehicle sales of 2.20 million units, with the Mustang being its only non-truck or SUV model. By
powertrain type, internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles accounted for 85.8% of its total U.S. vehicle sales
(1.89 million units), with hybrids accounting for 10.4% (228,000 units), and pure battery EVs for the remaining
3.8% (84,000 units). Just a few weeks ago, F appeared to have a lot of positive momentum, coming off a
year in which its U.S. vehicle sales jumped 6.0% in 2025, outperforming overall U.S. new vehicle sales growth
of 2.2%. Sales of F’s profit-driving F-series pickups and SUV models (e.g., Explorer, Expedition, and Bronco)
were poised to benefit from the combination of lower fuel prices and interest rates, but recent events have
clouded its outlook. The Detroit Three all rank at the bottom of the industry in terms of real-world fuel
economy, per the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).
EV manufacturers could benefit. When fuel prices spike, the economics of EVs become more attractive in
the eyes of consumers seeking to avoid higher prices at the pump. Investor sentiment toward EVs has been
depressed for several quarters now, particularly since it became clear that the U.S. government was going to
eliminate federal EV subsidies following the 2024 election. For smaller EV manufacturers, concerns related
to liquidity, balance sheet, and short interest have risen over this timeframe, and Q4 auto sales data showed
a steep drop in U.S. EV sales (-36 % Y/Y) following the federal EV tax credit expiration on September 30,
2025. Cash burn rates are high for smaller EV manufacturers (e.g., LCID, RIVN, PSNY), and short interest
remains elevated, with ~33% of the float for LCID, 18% for RIVN, 5% for PSNY, and 2% for TSLA. Meanwhile,
traditional automakers have announced the discontinuation of a number of EV models, reducing competition.
As the bestselling U.S. EV manufacturer and a company whose investment-grade balance sheet provides a
level of flexibility not afforded to many of their peers, we think TSLA could see an uptick in vehicle demand,
although the company’s vehicle portfolio is in somewhat of a transition phase and it is in the process of
phasing out the Model S and X. EV manufacturers are an automotive sub-group we believe would benefit
from higher oil prices, and high short interest creates conditions that are potentially ripe for a short squeeze.
Auto dealerships might not be as hurt by higher fuel prices as one might think. This is because dealers’
high-margin parts and service segments – not their relatively low-margin vehicle sales businesses - account
for the majority of their total gross profit. Parts and service results tend to be less cyclical, as demand is
primarily driven by vehicle repair and maintenance activity. While we would expect dealers’ new vehicle sales
to suffer in a higher fuel price environment and they would likely have to boost incentives to stimulate sales,
most dealers sell used vehicles as well, which we think could see some benefit from consumers trading
down.
Auto suppliers could be hurt as well. The financial results of auto parts & equipment companies are highly
correlated with automobile production rates. If there is a decline in new vehicle sales growth, auto
manufacturers could potentially cut production, which would hurt parts demand. Over the past several
decades, this has happened many times during industry downturns, with automakers often announcing plans
to reduce production shifts, idle factory lines, lay off workers, or shutter entire plants. While 2025
represented the domestic and global industry’s strongest year from a sales volume perspective since 2019,
current inventory levels are well above average, which we think places auto suppliers at much greater risk
than normal. Some of the larger-cap auto suppliers in our coverage universe include MGA, Aptiv PLC (APTV
73 *****), Autoliv Inc. (ALV 109 ****), and BorgWarner Inc. (BWA 53 ***).
A couple other big losers. RV manufacturers such as THO and WGO, which technically fall under the auto
manufacturers sub-industry, are some of the most exposed to changes in fuel prices. After U.S. RV sales hit
a record high of 660,240 units in 2021, before a precipitous decline over more than 50% to just 313,174 units
in 2023, the industry was believed to be in the early stages of a recovery, with the RV Industry Association
forecasting a 2.0% Y/Y sales volume increase to 349,000 units in 2026. The jump in oil prices clouds the
outlook for RV demand, a sub-group of products considered among the most discretionary in the sector.

Risk Factors
Risks to our analysis include, but are not limited to, global economic conditions given the highly cyclical
nature of the automotive industry, interest rates, crude oil and gasoline prices, and raw material and labor
costs. Currency rates could also be more or less favorable than we anticipate. Other risks to our thesis
include changes in domestic and global automobile supply and demand, the impact of tariffs and other
recent trade policy changes on financial results, and other economic and consumer-related factors,
including, but not limited to, GDP growth, inflation, consumer confidence, employment statistics, wages,
discretionary income, and crude oil/gasoline prices.
Company Implications
Ford Motor Company (F 12 ***) was the third-largest motor vehicle manufacturer in terms of U.S. sales
volume in 2025 behind General Motors Company (GM 77 ***) and TM, accounting for 13.2% of U.S. new
vehicle sales. F’s U.S. sales totaled 2.20 million vehicles (+6% Y/Y), comprising 1.89 million ICE vehicles,
228,072 hybrid vehicles, and 84,113 EVs. F’s key brands include Ford and Lincoln. We have a Hold opinion
and a 12-month price target of $16 on F shares, based on 9.1x our 2027 EPS estimate, an early-cycle
premium to the stock’s five-year average forward P/E of 8.1x. We believe the stock’s valuation is justified by
its strong position in the U.S. auto industry (particularly in the high-margin truck/ SUV market), attractive
dividend yield, strong balance sheet, and globally recognized brand. F’s large union exposure, lower market
share in non-U.S. countries, and long-term liabilities such as pension obligations are also important valuation
considerations.
Magna International Inc. (MGA 59 ***) is one of the world’s largest manufacturers of auto parts and
equipment. In 2025, MGA generated total revenue of $42.0 billion, down from $42.8 billion in 2024. We have
a Hold opinion and a 12-month price target of $75 on MGA. Our price target is based on a P/E of 10.6x our
2027 EPS estimate, a slight discount to the stock’s 10-year average forward P/E of 10.7x. We view its
valuation as justified by fundamental headwinds and competitive pressures in the space, broad and
diversified base of auto parts, high degree of customer concentration (six customers accounted for 74% of
total revenue in 2024), strong balance sheet, and cash returns to shareholders in the form of both dividends
and buybacks. Historically, auto parts & equipment companies have proven to be highly cyclical, with sales
and earnings reflecting auto production rates and other factors.
Rivian Automotive Inc. (RIVN 17 ***) is an EV pure play that was founded in 2009 and is headquartered in
California. In 2025, RIVN generated total revenue of $7.8 billion on global sales of 42,247 units of the
R1T/R1S in 2025, down from 51,579 units in 2024, and 50,122 units in 2023. We have a Hold opinion and a
12-month price target of $20 on RIVN. Our price target reflects a material premium to our DCF valuation and
RIVN’s tangible book value of $3.68/share as of December 31, 2025. As a company with no profitability in
sight, we see significant liquidity risk. However, we think RIVN’s balance sheet is in better shape than most
other upstart EV manufacturers. As of December 31, 2025, RIVN had $6.1 billion of cash and cash
equivalents and $5.0 billion of total debt, versus $7.7 billion of cash and $5.0 billion of debt on December 31,
2024. The auto industry is notorious for its low margins, high barriers to entry, and high failure rate. In recent
years, a number of other start-up EV manufacturers have declared bankruptcy, including Fisker Inc. and
Lordstown Motors.
Tesla Inc. (TSLA 401 **) is an EV pure play, with five non-commercial battery EV models currently available:
the Cybertruck, Model Y, Model 3, Model X, and Model S. In 2025, TSLA generated total revenue of $94.8
billion in global sales of approximately 1.64 million vehicles (-9% Y/Y), consisting of 1.59 million Model 3 and Y
vehicles and 50,850 for the Model S, X, Cybertruck, and Semi. The geographic breakdown of TSLA’s total
revenue in 2025 was: U.S. (50%), China (22%), and All Other (28%). TSLA has four reportable segments:
Automotive sales (72% of total 2025 revenues), Automotive Leasing (2%), Services & Other (13%), and
Energy Generation & Storage (13%). TSLA accounted for 46% of all U.S. EV sales in 2025, according to
Kelley Blue Book data. The Model Y and Model 3 were the two bestselling U.S. EV models by a wide margin

THOR Industries Inc. (THO 87 ***) is the world’s largest manufacturer of recreational vehicles. In FY 25 (Jul.),
THO generated total revenue of $9.6 billion on North American Towable RV sales of 119,790 units (+6%
Y/Y), North American Motorized RV sales of 17,153 units (-9%), and European RV sales of 44,445 units (-
20%). We have a Hold opinion and a 12-month price target of $100 on THO, representing a justified early-
cycle premium to THO’s 10-year average forward P/E of 15.2x and a material premium to RV manufacturer
peers. We believe the stock’s valuation is justified by the highly cyclical nature of the industry, the
discretionary nature of its products, geographic diversification (North America is 68% of FY 25 revenues and
Europe is 32%), and 100% non-union work force. The company generates gross margins similar to those of
most auto manufacturers.
Winnebago Industries Inc. (WGO 35 ****) is one of the world’s largest manufacturers of recreational vehicles.
In FY 25 (Aug.), WGO generated total revenue of $2.8 billion on Towable RV sales of 31,169 units (-3% Y/Y),
Motorhome RV sales of 5,742 units (-15%), and Boat sales of 4,635 units (+12%). We have a Buy opinion and
a 12-month price target of $60 on WGO. Our price target represents a justified early-cycle premium to
WGO’s 10-year average forward P/E of 12.4x and a material discount to RV manufacturer peers. We believe
the stock’s valuation is justified by the highly cyclical nature of the industry, the discretionary nature of its
products, lack of geographic diversification (U.S. represented 94% of net revenues in FY 25), and 100% non-
union workforce. The company generates gross margins similar to those of most auto manufacturers.
