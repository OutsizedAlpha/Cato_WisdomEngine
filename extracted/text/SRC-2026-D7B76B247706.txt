Energy prices have risen sharply since the Iran strikes began. The price of Brent crude
briefly surpassed $110pb for the first time since 2022 before falling back to ~$100pb.
Price of Brent Crude ($ per Barrel, Latest = 2nd April)
Sources: LSEG, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

Prices of oil products have risen more than the Brent and WTI benchmarks. The biggest
increases have been in Asia, while the smallest have been in the US.
% Change in Fuel Prices Since War Began (Latest = 2nd April)*
Sources: LSEG, Capital Economics *US = Gulf Coast. Europe = Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Antwerp region.
www.capitaleconomics.com

Natural gas prices have surged in Europe and Asia, while the US remains largely
unaffected.
Natural Gas Prices (2025 = 100, Latest = 2nd April)
Sources: LSEG, EIA, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

Asian economies are particularly reliant on natural gas supplies from the Middle East,
especially Pakistan, Taiwan and Korea.
Natural Gas (% of Energy Supply, 2024)
Sources: Energy Institute, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

Higher energy prices reflect the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, which accounts
for 25% and 20% of global seaborne oil and LNG shipments, respectively.
Trade via the Strait of Hormuz (% of Global Maritime Trade)
Sources: LSEG, EIA, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

Shipping traffic through the Strait has almost ground to a halt since the conflict began.
Average Daily Vessel Arrivals Into the Strait of Hormuz
Sources: IMF Portwatch, Capital Economics *Includes general cargo vessels and Ro-Ros. Given the conflict, reports of vessel GPS
jamming and AIS spoofing suggest latest figures should be considered low estimates.
www.capitaleconomics.com

Given the importance of the Middle East for energy trade, tanker freight rates have
trebled. In contrast, air freight and container freight rates have barely risen.
Global Shipping Costs (2025 = 100)
Sources: LSEG, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

The limited movement in container rates reflects the fact that the region has been a no-go
area for container ships since the Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping in late 2023.
Containership Traffic (Millions of TEUs, 30-day MA)
Sources: LSEG, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

High energy prices will weigh on activity in large energy importers in Europe and Asia.
The benefits to Middle East exporters would be limited by disruptions from the conflict.
Energy Trade Balance (% of own GDP, 2024)
Sources: ITC, LSEG, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

With most EM external balance sheets in a good place, there is limited risk of the energy
crisis triggering an EM-wide crisis.
CE Aggregate EM* Currency Crisis Risk Indicator
Sources: Capital Economics

But a few EMs remain vulnerable to currency crises, most notably Turkey, Egypt, Zambia
and Uganda.
Net Energy Export Balance (2024) & Currency Crisis Risk Indicator (Latest)

The outlook for energy prices is highly uncertain. In our baseline scenario, the conflict
proves short-lived and energy prices fall back.
CE Scenarios for Energy Prices

In the adverse scenario, lasting damage to infrastructure would mean that energy prices
stay high for a protracted period.

The largest impact of higher energy prices on inflation will be the direct effect on fuel and
piped gas prices. But there will also be indirect effects on the prices of transport and food.
Where will higher energy prices show up in the CPI?

Higher gas prices in Europe means that inflation will rise further there than in the US. In
Japan, generous fuel subsidies will limit the passthrough from wholesale prices.
Baseline Scenario Headline Inflation (%)
Sources: LSEG, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

In the baseline scenario, UK inflation would peak a little over 4%, while it could rise to 7%
in the adverse scenario.
UK CPI Inflation (%)
Sources: LSEG, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

Similarly, inflation in the euro-zone should rise to around 4% in the baseline and around
6% in the adverse scenario.
Euro-zone HICP Inflation (%)
Sources: LSEG, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

In the US, inflation will peak earlier at a little over 3% in the baseline, while it would
breach 4% in the adverse scenario.
US CPI Inflation (%)
Sources: LSEG, Capital Economics
www.capitaleconomics.com

Aggregate EM inflation will rise by a similar amount to DM inflation.

Fuel subsidies or price caps should limit the inflationary impact in several EMs. But in the

In our baseline scenario, global GDP growth would slow to below 2.5% y/y. But in the
adverse scenario, it would slow sharply and meet most definitions of a “global recession”.
