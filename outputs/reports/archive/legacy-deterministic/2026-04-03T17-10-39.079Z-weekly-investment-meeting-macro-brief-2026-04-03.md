# Weekly Investment Meeting Macro Brief

_Date window: March 30, 2026 through April 3, 2026. Compiled on April 3, 2026 at approximately 17:59 BST._

## Executive Summary

The macro week was not a clean growth-scare week. It was a mixed but investable combination of firmer U.S. activity and labor data, sticky geopolitical input-cost pressure, a still-improving but uneven European manufacturing picture, a respectable Japan Tankan, and a China/EM complex that looked better in official activity data than in the private-sector detail.

The U.S. was the single clearest upside surprise. March nonfarm payrolls printed 178K against a 60K consensus after a sharply negative revised February base, unemployment slipped to 4.3%, initial jobless claims stayed low at 202K, and ISM manufacturing improved to 52.7. Wage growth cooled to 0.2% month over month and 3.5% year over year, which softened the labor-cost message without making the release dovish in a broad sense. The trade deficit widened to $57.3 billion in February, but the broader activity message was still one of resilience rather than rollover.

Europe was more nuanced. Euro area flash CPI for March printed 2.5% year over year against 2.6% expected, while core slowed to 2.3% against 2.4% expected. That is mildly better than feared on core disinflation, but it came alongside manufacturing PMIs that continued to recover and with Germany still showing soft retail demand. France and Italy were near or above the manufacturing expansion threshold, but Spain fell back into contraction. The signal is not clean disinflationary weakness. It is more like improving industrial breadth colliding with geopolitically driven cost pressure.

The UK remained sluggish in hard growth terms but less comfortable on inflation psychology. Q4 2025 GDP was unrevised at 0.1% quarter over quarter and 1.0% year over year, while the current account deficit widened to 2.4% of GDP including precious metals. More importantly for the near-term policy tone, the Bank of England's March Decision Maker Panel showed single-month one-year-ahead CPI inflation expectations rising to 3.5% and own-price expectations rising to 3.7%, explicitly linked to recent energy-price increases.

Japan was not weak, but it was not a clean reflation boom either. The Bank of Japan's Tankan showed large manufacturers' business conditions rising to 17 from 16 and large non-manufacturers holding at 36, while large all-industry FY2026 capex plans came in at 3.3%. That is still positive, but it is not explosive, and it leaves Japan exposed if imported energy pressure intensifies.

China and selected emerging markets were bifurcated. China's official March manufacturing PMI rose back above 50 to 50.4 and the non-manufacturing PMI to 50.1, but the Caixin private-sector signal was softer than the official rebound and South Korea's data did more than China's private surveys to confirm ongoing external demand. Korea's March exports surged 48.3% year over year and manufacturing PMI rose to 52.6, although imports missed expectations and CPI rose only 2.2% year over year.

The practical conclusion for asset markets is that the growth side of the equation did not weaken enough this week to offset the inflationary and geopolitical impulse. In John Murphy terms, the commodity and geopolitical shock is still trying to push into bonds, currencies, and equity leadership before the growth data have cracked decisively.

## What Matters Most

### 1. The U.S. week was hawkish in growth terms, only partly offset by softer wages.

The payroll report was the dominant surprise. A 178K payroll gain against a 60K consensus is not a minor beat, especially after February's negative revised base. The unemployment rate slipping to 4.3% and claims remaining near 200K says the labor market is not signaling imminent recession. Wage growth cooled, which matters, but the larger read-through is that the Fed is still dealing with an economy that can absorb geopolitical noise better than the market had feared.

### 2. Europe is not giving a pure disinflation signal.

Euro area core inflation softened, which is the cleanest dovish input of the week, but headline CPI re-accelerated and the manufacturing cycle kept healing. Germany's retail sales miss says the consumer side is still soft, while Spain's PMI relapse says the industrial upturn is not uniform. In portfolio terms, that is a messier environment than a simple "Europe weak, ECB cuts" narrative.

### 3. The UK is vulnerable to imported inflation more than to domestic overheating.

The UK growth numbers were too soft to justify complacency, yet the BoE's firm-level survey showed rising CPI and own-price expectations tied to recent energy-price moves. That is precisely the kind of combination that can keep gilts under pressure even if the real economy itself is only trudging along.

### 4. Japan is steady, but energy sensitivity remains a live risk.

The Tankan did not crack. That matters. But capex plans were only moderate and the outlook softened from current conditions. Japan can tolerate a moderate global reflation pulse; it is much less comfortable if the pulse is being driven by imported oil and shipping stress rather than healthy domestic demand.

### 5. China/EM remains a two-speed story.

China's official PMIs recovered above 50, but the more market-sensitive message is that external Asia, especially Korea, still looks healthier than China's private-sector demand pulse. That matters because it keeps the global macro signal from becoming outright bearish, but it also warns against reading one official China PMI bounce as a durable all-clear.

## Intermarket Discussion In The Spirit Of John Murphy

Murphy's core discipline is to stop looking at each print in isolation and instead ask how commodities, bonds, currencies, and equities are talking to one another. On that framework, this week matters because the inflationary channels were trying to reassert themselves before the growth data cracked.

Start with commodities and geopolitics. The Middle East war and associated shipping and energy risks are not abstract background noise. They are the mechanism pushing up inflation expectations and input costs. The Bank of England's DMP explicitly pointed to recent energy-price increases as firms raised both CPI expectations and own-price expectations. That same dynamic is visible in the broader manufacturing complex: Europe recovered on activity, but the quality of that recovery is being complicated by cost pressure rather than helped by easier inputs.

Then move to bonds. If payrolls had missed badly and claims had rolled over, the rates market could have looked through the geopolitical cost shock as a transient tax on demand. Instead, the U.S. growth and labor data were good enough to make the market take the inflation transmission channel more seriously. Stronger payrolls, firmer ISM, and still-low claims do not validate an immediate recession trade. They increase the chance that front-end yields stay sticky or back up when oil and shipping stress are rising.

Currencies come next. A resilient U.S. data set plus geopolitical stress is usually dollar-supportive, especially when Europe and the UK are more exposed to imported energy than the U.S. is. Japan also remains vulnerable on this axis because its external energy dependence can turn a global commodity shock into a domestic terms-of-trade problem even if the Tankan itself looks fine.

Equities are the last stage of the chain. In this setup, broad equity indices can still hold up for a while because the data are not recessionary. But leadership tends to rotate. Energy, defense, and select commodity-linked exposures gain relative support. Rate-sensitive long-duration equities, consumer discretionary, and Europe/Japan cyclicals become more fragile if the market concludes that geopolitical inflation is pushing yields higher without delivering a clean upside growth impulse.

The key Murphy-style point is that the bond market has not been given a clean reason to dismiss the commodity shock. That is the hinge for everything else.

## Asset-Market Implications Right Now

### Rates

The U.S. data reduce the credibility of an immediate hard-landing narrative. Softer wages help at the margin, but payrolls, claims, and ISM together argue for a shallower easing path than a weak market tape alone might imply. In Europe, softer core CPI argues the other way, but headline CPI and input-cost risk complicate the dovish case. In the UK, imported inflation looks more dangerous than domestic growth looks strong, which is not a good mix for duration.

### FX

The dollar should remain supported by the combination of U.S. resilience and geopolitical stress. Sterling and the euro both face imported-energy vulnerability, and the yen is still hostage to the terms-of-trade channel even if Japanese domestic surveys are respectable. In EMFX, Korea's export strength is constructive, but broader EM still becomes more fragile if oil and global yields continue higher together.

### Equities

This is not yet a "sell everything" macro tape. It is more of a leadership and quality regime. U.S. domestically resilient sectors can still work. Europe is harder because the manufacturing rebound is colliding with input-cost pressure. A commodity and defense tilt makes more sense than an indiscriminate cyclical-beta tilt if geopolitical stress remains elevated.

### Commodities and Credit

Oil and shipping remain the most important macro accelerants outside the data calendar itself. If energy pressure persists, inflation breakevens and credit spreads become the channels to watch. The data this week did not create that risk; they reduced the market's ability to ignore it.

## Bottom Line For Tomorrow's Meeting

The highest-quality conclusion from this week is not that the world has become cleanly stronger or weaker. It is that the macro tape remains resilient enough that geopolitical inflation pressure still matters. The U.S. did not give the market a recessionary escape hatch. Europe improved on activity but not in a clean enough way to relax about inflation transmission. The UK became incrementally more awkward for policy. Japan stayed stable but exposed. China recovered officially, while external Asia gave the better trade signal.

For portfolio discussion, the right question is not simply whether growth is rolling over. It is whether the next move in markets is driven by renewed inflation-premium pressure coming from geopolitics while growth is still decent enough to keep bond markets from fully rallying.
